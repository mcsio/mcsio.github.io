Post-mortem  ·  concentrated equity book

Seventy percent of my drawdown was money I deployed after the peak.

I lost 20.3% of a concentrated, unlevered semiconductor book over eighteen sessions. Almost none of that loss came from the position I was famous for defending. It came from the buying I did on the way down.

The part I got right

I run a concentrated, unlevered position in AI-adjacent semiconductors. That concentration is the thesis, not a risk I'm trying to manage away. I hold through drawdowns that shake most people out, and I'm unlevered specifically so that nobody can ever force me to sell at the bottom.

All of that worked. Through the entire episode, zero of my four written falsifiers printed. The thesis was intact on the last day of the drawdown in exactly the way it was intact on the first. If holding had been the only thing I did, this would be a boring story about a 20% mark-to-market on a volatile book.

It isn't, because holding wasn't the only thing I did.

The finding

Three weeks in, I stopped and did the attribution properly — not "which names went down," but which dollars lost the money. The answer reorganized the whole system.

A second, deeper post-mortem five days later put the post-peak share at 74%. The number moved a little; the conclusion didn't move at all.

This is not a stock-picking failure. Every name I bought at the top is a name I still want to own. It is a pacing failure, and pacing is a category of mistake that essentially no risk framework in retail investing is built to catch.

Here is the uncomfortable part. No price-structure rule fires at an all-time high. Not a stop, not a trailing stop, not a trend filter, not a moving-average gate, not a drawdown brake. Every one of them is silent — by construction — on the exact day you do the most damage. The peak is the one moment when the entire genre of risk rule has nothing to say.

The only rule that catches it is a cap on how fast you're allowed to buy. Nothing else even looks at the failure mode.

What I rebuilt

The old document had thirty-five rules. It had thirty-five rules because every time something went wrong I appended a patch, and patches never get deleted. Three of its dollar levels were stale — computed off a peak that no longer existed — including a hedge-harvest trigger sitting below the buy it was supposed to fund. On the worst day of the drawdown, its standing-stance section told a panicked reader to "deploy into strength."

The replacement has twelve rules, one precedence stack, and four invariants. These are the eight design decisions I'd carry into any version of this, for any book:

R3(a)

Label the money before you write any rules

Every lot carries one of two labels: conviction or trade. Trade lots have mechanical stops with no override, ever. Conviction lots get judgment.

The catch that makes it work: promotion to conviction requires a dated note naming the specific falsifiers that would exit that position. No written falsifiers, no conviction label, regardless of how strongly you feel about it. Demotion is free.

Origin A six-figure position acquired over eight sessions received conviction treatment purely by drift. Nobody ever decided it was conviction. It just never got a stop.

R2

Falsifiers govern the core; price gets three narrow valves

Four things can make me wrong, each written down in advance with its pre-agreed action — how much, which lots, on what clock. Leaving on a tripped falsifier is the plan working, green or red.

What explicitly does not exit the core: red days, flush days, a 10% down close, a scary alert, or the urge itself. A mark-to-market is not evidence. Price still reduces exposure at the edges through three bounded mechanical valves — but the core answers only to falsifiers.

R7

Cap the pace, with no signal exception

No more than 8% of the book bought in a session, no more than 15% in any rolling five. All buying counts — including tickets the system itself generated.

This is the rule that would have prevented most of the loss, and it is the one that is hardest to accept, because on the day it binds you will have an excellent reason to override it. That's the point. A paper cap loses every collision by default, so this one is machine-checked and a breach locks the buy gates for the next session.

R3(d)

Pins move one way — up

The reference price each stop measures against can be raised, never quietly lowered. A silently-lowered pin is ignored by the monitor and flagged once a day. Lowering one deliberately requires an explicit flag in the file and a dated line in the doctrine.

If this document only gives you one thing, take this one. One-way pins are what make a stop un-gameable from inside a drawdown, which is the only time anyone ever wants to game it.

Origin I lowered pins repeatedly across three weeks as the book fell. Each individual adjustment was defensible. Together they silenced the monitor for the six sessions immediately preceding the worst leg of the rout.

R6

Latch the counter-trend buy, then wait for confirmation

When the book crosses a weakness rung, the buy ticket latches: its composition fixes at that print. But it doesn't deploy until the sector confirms — two consecutive closes above a trend line — and each name only participates once it clears its own.

The first version of this fired and deployed the next morning, straight into the knife; the book fell five more points in two sessions. Latching preserves the counter-cyclical instinct — you commit at the discount — while the confirm stops you from catching the falling half of it. The cost is a slightly worse average fill. It is worth it.

P1 – P7

One precedence stack, looked up, never improvised

When two rules conflict — and in a real drawdown they conflict constantly — there is exactly one ordering that decides, and it lives in one place. Lower number wins. There is no exceptions clause anywhere else in the document.

Ambiguity in a rulebook doesn't produce caution. It produces whichever action you already wanted to take.

INVARIANT

Rules, not state

The document contains no dollar levels, no positions, no current posture — only percentages and conditions. Every live number is machine-written into a separate state file and rendered fresh each day.

Hand-maintained numbers inside a rulebook go stale silently, and they go stale fastest in exactly the conditions that make them load-bearing. Separating rules from state is what made the twelve-rule version possible at all.

INVARIANT

Adding a rule requires deleting a rule

Hard cap. Amendments rewrite the affected rule in place plus one dated changelog line — never an appended patch block.

Amendment stacking is how thirty-five rules happened, and a thirty-five-rule document is functionally a zero-rule document, because nobody can hold it in their head at the moment it's needed. Mine now has a five-line wallet card and a ten-step panic page. If it doesn't fit on a card, it won't survive contact with a bad morning.

What it costs

Every rule here has a price, and I made myself write each one down next to the rule it belongs to. A rulebook that only lists benefits is marketing, and you will abandon it the first time it hurts — which will be soon, because these rules are designed to hurt in exactly the moments they're working.

The two smaller lessons

Alerts that are never actionable train you to ignore alerts that are. On one representative day, eleven intraday stop-breach messages fired and zero of them were actionable — holding was correct on all eleven. Now every core decision is close-basis by construction and intraday alerts are explicitly labelled attention-only. An alert you've learned to swipe away is worse than no alert, because it costs you the reflex.

Hedge the factor you actually own. For weeks I held index puts against a sector book. Over the drawdown, the index fell 3.6% while my book fell 16.6%. The hedge was real, expensive, and pointed at the wrong thing. Every roll is now a full re-underwrite — basis, structure, and size re-derived from the live book — because "same structure, further out" is exactly how a hedge on the wrong factor perpetuates itself for a year.

What I'm actually asking

The system above exists as running code — nightly evaluation, machine-checked gates, an alert path, an auditable changelog with a ratification step. It took a year and a bad drawdown to build, and it was built entirely for one book: mine.

I don't know whether that's a product or a personal obsession, and I'd rather find out by asking than by spending six months building.

Do you have written rules for when you sell — and did you write them before or after you needed them?

If you have a real book and no written rules, I'd like fifteen minutes to hear how you decide. If you do have written rules, I'd like fifteen minutes even more, because I want to know what yours catch that mine don't.
